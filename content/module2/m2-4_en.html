The Federal Government

Powers of the Federal Government

The federal level of government has power over matters affecting Canada as a whole, such as national defence, criminal law, central bank and monetary policy, foreign relations, passports, and postal service.

Some areas come under shared federal and provincial jurisdiction. The federal government controls national transportation matters such as regulations related to aviation, marine and rail transport. In contrast, the provinces have jurisdiction over provincial highways, vehicle registration, and driver licensing.

Branches of the Federal Government

The federal government has three branches:

  • Judicial branch: the Courts
  • Legislative: the Crown, the Senate and the House of Commons
  • Executive: the Crown, the Prime Minister and the Cabinet.

Judicial Branch

The judicial branch interprets and applies the law. It includes judges and the courts and operates independently from the other branches of government.

The Supreme Court has the final decision on all constitutional questions and on important civil and criminal law cases sent up on appeal by the lower courts.

The Governor General, on the advice of the Governor-in-Council (the Federal Cabinet) makes all judicial appointments to the superior courts (trial or appellate) in each province or territory. The governor general also appoints judges on the advice of the Governor-in-Council to other superior courts which have jurisdiction for all of Canada: the Federal Court, the Federal Court of Appeal, the Tax Court of Canada, and the Supreme Court of Canada, which is the final court of appeal for all Canadian courts.

Legislative Branch

The legislative branch, also known as Parliament, is seen as the highest political institution in Canada's political system. It is the lawmaking branch of government. It includes the Crown (represented by the governor general), the Senate, and the House of Commons.

This branch provides a forum for debating leading political issues, and has the power and responsibility to create laws.

The Queen and the Governor General

The Queen is the Head of State and the governor general is her representative in Canada.

The governor general ensures that Canada always has a Prime Minister. The governor general also summons Parliament, opens and closes sessions of Parliament, reads the Speech from the Throne, gives Royal Assent to bills, signs state documents, and dissolves Parliament for an election.

The Senate

The Senate is the Upper House of Parliament. Senators are appointed by the governor general upon recommendation of the Prime Minister. They are selected to represent all regions of Canada. Currently, there are 105 seats in the Senate.

Senators discuss and vote on legislation after it passes in the House of Commons. No bill can become law unless it has been passed by the Senate.

The House of Commons

The House of Commons is the elected component of Parliament, consisting of 338 members. Members of the government sit in and answer to the House of Commons. Most major government legislation is introduced in the House. Legislation concerned with the raising or spending of funds is introduced in the House. The House is also the place where members of Parliament (MPs) hold the government to account, discuss national issues, and represent constituents’ views.

Executive Branch

The executive branch enacts, applies, and enforces all federal laws created by Parliament. It is composed of the Queen (represented by the governor general), the Cabinet, and the public service.

The administration includes all government departments, the armed forces, Crown corporations, and other bodies under the authority of the government.

The Prime Minister

The Prime Minister is the head of government and chair of the Cabinet. The Prime Minister's main areas of responsibility include:

  • Providing leadership and direction to the government;
  • Organizing the Cabinet, including the composition of portfolios and mandates;
  • Recommending the appointment of individuals to key positions.

The current Prime Minister is the Right Honourable Justin Trudeau, P.C., M.P.

The Cabinet

The Prime Minister appoints members of the Cabinet. These ministers oversee government operations and support government decision making. The Cabinet is organized into a number of different committees, which have different mandates and membership.

Read more about the members of the Cabinet.

In selecting the Cabinet ministers, the Prime Minister may take into account a variety of factors such as regional representation, language, sex, diversity, and specific expertise in a given area, such as finance. This helps ensure that the Cabinet represents a diversity of perspectives across the country.

Elections

The governor general, on the advice of the Prime Minister, has the power to dissolve Parliament and call an election. The Constitution Acts of 1867 and 1982 set the maximum time between federal general elections at five years, except in time of real or apprehended war, invasion or insurrection.

A new government is formed after a general election, when candidates from different political parties are elected from across Canada to serve their constituents as Members of Parliament.

The leader of the party that wins the largest number of seats in the House of Commons is asked by the governor general to form the new government and becomes Prime Minister.
