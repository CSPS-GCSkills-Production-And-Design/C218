The Public Service

The Public Service of Canada is the largest component of the executive branch. Public servants work to translate the declarations and definitions of public policy into action. As a public servant, you have a duty to loyally implement the public policy direction of the elected government of the day. You must give honest and impartial advice and make all information relevant to a decision available to ministers, but once the ministers have made lawful decisions, you must loyally implement them.

Departments and Agencies

Departments

Ministers are served by departments staffed with public servants. Departments execute the government’s policies and responsibilities through the application of laws, regulations, services, and programs.

Read more about the different departments.

Common Service Organizations

A common service organization or department is designated as a central supplier of particular services to support the requirements of departments. This designation is outlined in the Common Services Policy. Common service organizations include the Canada School of Public Service, Public Services and Procurement Canada, Employment and Social Development Canada, Global Affairs Canada, Justice Canada, the Public Service Commission of Canada, Statistics Canada, and the Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat.

Read about the Common Services Policy.

Central Agencies

Central agencies have a central coordinating role. The central agencies are the Prime Minister’s Office (PMO), the Privy Council Office (PCO), the Department of Finance, and the Treasury Board Secretariat (TBS). They work together to support Cabinet decision making and coordinate government initiatives.

Read more about the Role of Central Agencies.

The Clerk of the Privy Council

The Privy Council Office (PCO) is led by the Clerk of the Privy Council, the most senior federal public servant. The PCO assists the Clerk in providing professional, non-partisan support to the Prime Minister in the Prime Minister’s responsibilities as head of government, including management of senior-level appointments. The Clerk of the Privy Council has three primary roles: deputy minister to the Prime Minister, Secretary to the Cabinet, and head of the public service. In effect, the Privy Council Office is the Prime Minister’s department, and the Clerk its deputy minister.

Find out more about the Clerk of the Privy Council.

Agents of Parliament

Agents of Parliament provide service on behalf of Parliament. They are responsible directly to Parliament rather than to the government or a federal minister. This emphasizes their independence from the government of the day. They carry out duties assigned by statute and report to one or both houses of Parliament.

Examples of agents include the Office of the Auditor General, the Office of the Commissioner of Official Languages, the Office of the Information Commissioner of Canada, and the Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada.

Crown Corporations

Crown corporations function like government-owned companies. Some are financially independent. Others partly rely on the financial support of government for their operation and are subject to the Financial Administration Act. They are operated by boards of directors whose members are named by the government; some are expected to pay dividends to the Crown. Treasury Board reviews their corporate plans.
